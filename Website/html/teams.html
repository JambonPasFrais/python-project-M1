Cleveland Cavaliers

We choose to plot the rank evolution of the team for the 10 last years, in this way, we can see the evolution of team’s performance year after year.
The utilisation of an evolution plot was obvious cause it’s the best way to quickly have a view of the variation of a same variable (we also inverted the y axis for a better reading).
This plot allows us to have an idea of the capacity of a team to improve, stay or decrease in the NBA league.
The correlation plot helps us to see which variables can be useful to combine in a plot and which variables have an impact on other.
If the correlation between 2 variables is - 1 < corr(x,y) < -0.5 or 0.5 < corr(x,y) < 1, it’s a good indicator to have a deeper look on them. On the plot, we only show variables with the better correlation. For instance, we can see a huge correlation between the number of 2 points attempts and the number of 2 points scored.
This shows us that when a team try to score 2 points, most of the time, they succeed. But the correlation is lower for 3 points attempts and 3 points scored, we can conclude that the team should train their 3 points to have a better 3 points scored percentage or, when they have the choice, they should always choose to try 2 points.
We have also created a bar plot to take a look at the numbers of 2 points and 3 points scored during a complete season. The bar plot is the plot to model a quantity. This plot must be completed with the first plot (rank evolution) to allow us to deduct if the number of 2 and 3 points scored have a real impact on the final rank. In the future, we can also deduct if it’s efficient to be offensive.
The last plot is a spider chart where we can see the main defensive data: OREB, DREB, TOV, STL, BLK.
The plot represents those data for last 3 years to see the differences in the defence efficiency over the years.
This plot has also to be put in comparison with the bar plot and the evolution plot, to know if the defensive strategy is better than the offensive one.
